# Top 5 Paramotor Fatality Causes: What the Data Tells Us

**Published: March 2026**

![Powerlines](intro1.jpg)

Paramotoring is one of the most accessible forms of powered flight, but like all aviation, it carries inherent risks. At PPG Incidents, we've analyzed more than 450 accidents to identify the most common causes of death in our sport.

<!-- more -->

## The Data Behind This Analysis

This article is based on data from the [PPG Incidents Database](https://ppg-incidents.org), focusing specifically on fatal incidents where the cause was identified with high or maximum confidence. By understanding these patterns, we hope to help pilots recognize dangerous situations and make better decisions.


---

## #5: Powerline and Ground Object Collisions (6% combined)

![Powerlines](powerlines0.jpg)

Powerlines are one of the most feared hazards in paramotoring, and for good reason. They're difficult to see, hard to avoid, and contact with them is almost always catastrophic.


**What makes this dangerous:**
Powerlines are often invisible until it's too late. Pilots focusing on landing spots or navigating in unfamiliar terrain may not spot lines until they're in the danger zone. The combination of high voltage, the pilot being strapped into a metal frame, and the inability to quickly escape makes these accidents particularly deadly.

**How to protect yourself:**
- Always scout landing areas before flying
- Avoid low altitude flights over unfamiliar areas

---

## #4: Water Landings (7% of fatalities)

![Water landing](water1.jpg)

Water landings present a unique and deadly challenge. While they may seem less dangerous than hitting solid ground, the combination of the motor's weight and the risk of entanglement makes water particularly hazardous.

**What makes this dangerous:**
The weight of a paramotor (typically 25-35 kg) combined with the wing and lines creates a deadly trap. Cold water shock can impair swimming ability, and the pilot is often disoriented after an unexpected water entry. Even experienced swimmers can struggle when tangled in lines and weighed down by equipment.

**How to protect yourself:**
- Avoid low passes over water, especially in challenging conditions
- If flying over water, use a flotation device attached to aparamotor
- Right before landing - unstrap yourself everywhere except one leg strap
- Do not flare - allow wing to overshoot you to reduce risk of entanglement

## #3: Hardware Failure (12% of fatalities)

![Hardware](hardware1.jpg)

While paramotors are generally reliable machines, hardware failures do occur and can be catastrophic when they happen in flight.

**What makes this dangerous:**
Structural failures - particularly of carabiners, frames, or suspension systems - can lead to immediate loss of control. Engine failures, while usually manageable, can become fatal if they occur at low altitude, in turbulent conditions, or when the pilot panics and makes poor decisions.

**How to protect yourself:**
- Perform thorough preflight inspections every single flight
- Replace carabiners and critical components on schedule, not when they fail
- Inspect lines and wing for wear regularly
- Practice engine-out scenarios at safe altitudes
- Know your glide ratio and always have landing options within glide

## #2: Turbulence (24% of fatalities)

![Turbulence](turbulence1.jpg)

Turbulence-related fatalities represent nearly a quarter of all deaths in our database. These incidents often involve a chain of events: turbulence causes a collapse, the pilot reacts incorrectly, and the situation spirals out of control.

**What makes this dangerous:**
Turbulence can strike unexpectedly, even on seemingly calm days. A collapse close to the ground leaves little time for recovery. The danger multiplies when pilots are already flying near terrain, performing maneuvers, or in conditions beyond their skill level.

**How to protect yourself:**
- Never fly in conditions beyond your skill level
- Understand different types of turbulence: rotor, thermal, wind shear, gust fronts
- Avoid flying in the lee of obstacles (buildings, trees, hills)
- Be especially cautious during thermal hours
- Give yourself altitude margin - it's your insurance policy
- Learn active piloting techniques for collapse recovery

## #1: Wrong Control Input (43% of fatalities)

![Wrong control](wrong_control1.jpg)

The leading cause of death in paramotoring is pilot error - specifically, **incorrect control inputs** that initiate a dangerous situation.

**What makes this dangerous:**

The data reveals a clear pattern: **80% of wrong control input fatalities occurred during active maneuvers**. When pilots intentionally perform aerobatic moves - spirals, wingovers, or other maneuvers - they enter a high-risk zone where any mistake can be fatal.

**Spirals / autorotaions are the deadliest scenarios**, involved in 56% of these fatalities. Spirals can quickly escalate beyond the pilot's ability to recover, especially at low altitude. G-forces can cause the pilot to black out, and the descent rate becomes extreme. Many pilots enter a spiral unintentionally and don't recognize it until it's too late to recover.

**Student pilots were involved in 16% of these incidents** - while this is a contributing factor (experience level) rather than a specific wrong control action, it highlights the danger of inexperience combined with overconfidence or panic.

**Wrong throttle management accounts for 12%** of these fatalities. For example, releasing throttle abruptly can cause a wing surge and subsequent collapse - a mistake that becomes deadly at low altitude.

**Other specific wrong actions include:** Medical issues causing loss of control (12%), Line twists from incorrect brake inputs (8%), Oscillations out of control (8%), Stall induced by aggressive brake application (4%)

**How to protect yourself:**
- Build your skills progressively - don't attempt maneuvers you haven't trained for
- Practice spiral entries and exits at high altitude with an instructor
- Always leave yourself altitude margin for recovery
- Check your trimmer settings before every flight
- Use brake toggles with secure attachments
- If you're a student, fly within your limits and with proper supervision
- Consider SIV (Safety In Flight) training to practice recovery techniques

## Conclusion: The Path to Safer Flying

![Safe fly](safe_fly.jpg)

The data is clear: most paramotor fatalities are preventable. They result from:

1. **Flying beyond your skill level** - especially performing maneuvers without proper training
2. **Insufficient altitude** - eliminating your safety margin
3. **Poor decision-making** - flying in unsuitable conditions or locations
4. **Equipment neglect** - failing to maintain and inspect your gear

The good news is that these are all within your control. Every pilot can choose to:
- Build skills progressively with proper instruction
- Maintain altitude margins
- Make conservative decisions about when and where to fly
- Keep equipment in top condition

Paramotoring can be a remarkably safe activity when approached with respect, proper training, and good judgment. Learn from those who came before - their stories, preserved in our database, contain lessons that could save your life.
